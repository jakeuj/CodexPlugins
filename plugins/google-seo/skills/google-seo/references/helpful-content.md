## Core Principle

Google says its automated ranking systems aim to show helpful, reliable information made to benefit people, not content made mainly to manipulate search rankings.

Use this reference as a content-quality lens after crawl/index blockers are handled, or alongside Search Console data when traffic drops may be content-related.

## Review Order

1. Start with affected pages and queries when Search Console data exists.
2. Identify the intended audience and the page's job.
3. Evaluate whether the page gives that audience a satisfying answer or next step.
4. Compare the page against competing search results for originality, completeness, usefulness, and trust.
5. Separate content problems from technical SEO problems in the final report.

## Content And Quality Questions

Check whether the page:

- Provides original information, reporting, research, analysis, or first-hand insight.
- Covers the topic substantially enough for its stated intent.
- Adds meaningful value instead of merely copying, summarizing, or lightly rewriting other sources.
- Uses clear titles that summarize the content without exaggeration or clickbait.
- Feels polished, edited, and cared for rather than rushed or mass-produced.
- Gives users more value than the other pages they are likely to find for the same query.
- Is useful enough that a reader might bookmark, share, recommend, cite, or trust it as a reference.

## Expertise, Trust, E-E-A-T, And YMYL

Use E-E-A-T as a quality lens, not as a mechanical ranking field to optimize.

- Trust is the most important part; experience, expertise, and authority support trust.
- Look for clear sources, evidence, author/site background, review process, and signs that the creator understands the topic.
- Flag obvious factual errors, unsupported claims, or missing context.
- Apply stricter scrutiny to YMYL topics that can affect health, finances, safety, society, welfare, or other high-impact decisions.
- Remember search quality raters do not directly control rankings; their guidelines are useful for understanding quality expectations.

## People-First Signals

Treat these as positive signs:

- The site has an existing or intended audience that would find the content useful if they came directly to the site.
- The content clearly shows first-hand experience or deep knowledge.
- The site has a clear main purpose or focus.
- Readers can leave with enough understanding to achieve their goal.
- Readers are likely to feel satisfied rather than needing to search again immediately.

## Search-Engine-First Warning Signs

Flag these as risks:

- Content exists mainly to attract search visits rather than help the site's audience.
- The site publishes large amounts of content on many topics hoping some of it ranks.
- Automation or AI is used at scale mainly to manipulate rankings.
- The page aggregates what others say without adding much value.
- The topic was chosen only because it appears popular, not because it serves the audience.
- The page leaves readers needing another source for a complete answer.
- Content length targets are based on a supposed Google word-count preference.
- The site enters a niche without real expertise mainly for search traffic.
- The page promises answers that are not actually known or confirmed.
- Dates are changed to make content look fresh without meaningful updates.
- Large amounts of old content are added or removed merely to make the site look fresh.

## Page Experience

Do not reduce page experience to one metric. Evaluate whether the overall page is usable, accessible, stable, and pleasant enough to support the content's purpose.

## Who, How, And Why

Use this framework for transparency recommendations:

- `Who`: Make it easy for visitors to understand who created the content. Recommend bylines, author pages, organization background, or reviewer notes when useful.
- `How`: Explain the process when it helps trust, such as testing methods, data sources, review process, or the role of automation and AI.
- `Why`: The best reason to create content is to help people. If the main reason is search traffic or ranking manipulation, treat it as a content quality risk.

For AI-assisted or automated content, do not require disclosure on every page by default. Recommend disclosure when users would reasonably wonder how the content was made, when automation materially shaped the output, or when explaining the process improves trust.

## Reporting Guidance

- Lead with the user impact, not a vague "SEO quality" label.
- Cite page-level evidence: affected URL, query intent, missing information, unsupported claim, duplicated section, weak title, stale date, or unclear author/process.
- Recommend improving, consolidating, redirecting, noindexing, or removing content only when the page-level evidence supports that action.
- Avoid broad pruning or date refreshes as ranking tactics.
- Do not promise rankings or recovery timelines; frame changes as improving usefulness, clarity, trust, and crawlable understanding.
